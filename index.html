Frequently asked

Answers for specifiers, sustainability teams, and product engineers.

What is the CDPH Standard Method and do you test to v1.2?
Yes. The California Department of Public Health (CDPH) Standard Method v1.2 — originally California Section 01350 — is the VOC emissions test required for LEED Low-Emitting Materials credit and most state-level low-VOC procurement. Berkeley Analytical has run this method since its inception and is listed on the CDPH Environmental Health Laboratory Branch website as a qualified provider.
How long does a full VOC emissions test take?
CDPH v1.2 requires 10 days of sample conditioning followed by a 96-hour small-scale chamber test. End-to-end, plan on roughly 3 weeks from sample arrival to final report. Expedited and parallel-sample scheduling is available.
Do you test biomedical devices to ISO 18562-3?
Yes. We perform ISO 18562-3:2024 VOC analysis of breathing-gas pathways for ventilators, CPAP/BiPAP systems, anesthesia equipment, and other respiratory devices. Reports are structured to support regulatory submissions.
Can your reports be used directly for LEED and BIFMA submissions?
Yes. Our reports are written to drop directly into LEED v4/4.1 Low-Emitting Materials credit documentation, BIFMA e3 product credits, FloorScore, WELL, CHPS, Green Globes, CALGreen, and ASHRAE 189.1 submissions. We include the test method, raw concentrations, modeled exposure, and pass/fail determination clearly labeled.
Are you ISO/IEC 17025 accredited?
Yes. Berkeley Analytical (laboratory TL-383) is accredited by IAS (International Accreditation Service) under AC89 and ISO/IEC 17025:2017. Our current accreditation is effective as of October 28, 2024.
What's the difference between VOC content and VOC emissions?
VOC content (ASTM D6886, D6007) measures how much VOC is in the product itself — relevant for paint and coating regulations like AIM rules. VOC emissions (CDPH, BIFMA, ISO 16000-9) measure what the product actually releases into indoor air over time — relevant for occupant health, LEED, and BIFMA. Most green-building programs care about emissions.
